Paycrave

mobile app

Problem

Several mobile payment solutions exist currently, like Square Cash or PayPal mobile. However, when it comes to food trucks, a well-designed solution hasn’t been created.

 

Solution

Paycrave is a crave-inspired mobile application that connects eaters with 4-wheel chefs and allows a user to discover nearby food trucks, view the menu, select items for purchase, and order food straight from their phone.

Branding

I worked with Paycrave’s existing identity elements—color, typography, logo to create an experience that supported the brand attributes: whimsical, modern, and young.

Discovery

I found the two patterns that informed my design choices: Panera bread and Yelp. Panera bread has an effortless check-out system. Yelp, of course, sets the standard for reviews and ratings. One of my design philosophies is to make it simple for the user; ease is achieved when we use established patterns.

User Survey

To delve into the target market, I created a user survey to ask about food truck experiences, preferences and what drove decision making. The bulk of the 25 respondents chose the following features as the important ones: viewing a map/a list of food truck nearby, menus, reading reviews, using app without logging in, and payment options. These survey results formed the basis of a user flow creation.

Personas

The food truck enthusiasts would like to have a clean app experience that was, first of all, about locating food in a timely manner. I created a persona to guide design decisions, priorities, and create empathy.

User Flows

I created user flows to show how a user follows through Paycrave interface to complete certain tasks from app launch to payment confirmation and reviews. Being able to see the user’s journey through the app helped me to visualize each screen layout.

 

This project was my first mobile application. So the most difficult and at the same time the most important part of it was a user flow creating. First of all, I made sketches on a paper and after that implemented it on Drow.io. My issue was to think through all steps of app, but thanks to my mentor I managed it. She found some mistakes, after their correcting, the user flow was ready.

Sketches

I started the wireframe process with a pen and paper. Through these sketches I was able to determine the general layout of the various app screens and also to explore how navigation would feel and look  if a tab bar was used.

Mockups

I created high-fidelity wireframes and mockups using Sketch3. It was easy to do due to the user flow. Despite the lack of need to come up with a super creative idea or a complicated collage, my problem was to design a simple, intuitive and pleasing-to-the-eye application. I chose a "minimize learning" approach to support users on-the-go, so they could use the app without effort. I designed the Home Page to default like a Map-view of all food trucks nearby. In addition, switch between Map-view and List-view allowed users to get different visual information about each food truck by proximity.

Once a user decides on a specific food truck to order from, they can get directions, view an overall cuisine and food truck’s hours, and read reviews. All this information helps a user decide if they want to place an order. Then users can go to the menus, choose one or more meals and add it to their cart. After a review of the order users can proceed to checkout.

Users can create and manage their account, and browse their order history. Users can also write a review for each specific food truck after their order has been picked up.

User Testing

I conducted a usability test with seven participants. Key tasks I tested were: choosing a food truck to follow; ordering and paying; reviewing. Based on feedback I prioritized my changes.

view prototype

Conclusion

This project was a good first try at designing for a mobile app and showed me to how much size is incredibly important for mobile. I realized that for testing the clickable prototype I had to increase the font size of nearly all of my text and a white space. Paycrave was also a great exercise in learning how to design for a preset brand. However, I’m not sure that app might be successful. By their nature, food trucks are limited in their ability to handle large orders. Adding mobile orders might slow the process and drive off clientele. So Paycrave might be an app for finding trucks and a platform through which users and participating trucks can build loyalty. Creating an account with Paycrave allows users to save dietary preferences, log favorite trucks, and post ratings. Food trucks can set discounts for their regular customers.
